# Reference — Life Coaching Business Model

> Domain knowledge for the discovery agent. Read this before any session so you arrive with working assumptions about how coaching businesses typically operate. Use this to ask informed questions, not blank-slate ones.

## How Life Coaching Businesses Are Structured

Most solo life coaches run a service business with 3–4 revenue streams:

**1. One-on-one coaching** — the core offering. Usually sold as:
- Single sessions (typically $150–$400/session)
- Packages (e.g., 3-month, 6-month engagements — 4–8 sessions)
- Monthly retainers with a set number of sessions included

**2. Group programs or cohorts** — run less frequently, higher leverage. Often theme-specific (career, leadership, etc.)

**3. Licensed or certified programs** — like the Positive Intelligence Program®. These have external structure, required materials, and licensing constraints. The coach delivers the program but does not own the IP.

**4. Speaking, workshops, or training** — corporate or organizational engagements, often a lead source as well as a revenue stream

Most solo coaches have 5–15 active clients at any one time. At the high end (premium pricing), 5–8 clients is full capacity. At mid-market pricing, 12–20 is possible but typically unsustainable solo.

## How Solopreneurs Run the Back Office (Reality, Not Ideal)

This is what solo coaches actually do — not what they say they do:

**Lead tracking:** Usually no real CRM. Common substitutes: a Google Sheet, a notebook, an email folder, or nothing. Leads are often tracked by memory for coaches with low volume.

**Scheduling:** Almost always a scheduling tool (Calendly is the most common). But what happens *around* the scheduling tool is often manual — the follow-up email, the reminder, the rescheduling.

**Contracts:** Common tools include DocuSign, HelloSign, or a PDF sent by email and returned signed. Some coaches use coaching-specific platforms (Paperbell, CoachAccountable) that handle agreements.

**Payment:** Stripe and PayPal are most common. Invoicing is often done manually or through a simple invoicing tool. Some coaches take payment upfront per package; others invoice monthly.

**Session notes:** Almost always informal. Common approaches: a Google Doc per client, handwritten notes, a Notion page, or a Word document. Rarely standardized. Often not shared with clients.

**Between-session communication:** Varies widely. Common channels: email, WhatsApp, Telegram, or a client portal within a platform. For many solo coaches this is ad hoc — only when someone reaches out, not on a proactive schedule.

**Client tracking:** Typically a document or spreadsheet where the coach records what each client committed to in the last session and what the current focus area is. Often inconsistent.

## Common Pain Points in Solo Coaching Practices

These come up in almost every discovery session with a solo coach. Use them as hypotheses to test, not assumptions to state:

1. **Post-session follow-up slips** — the coach intends to send a summary or next-step email but it gets delayed or skipped. Common because it requires customization and there's always something more urgent.

2. **Lead follow-up gaps** — someone expresses interest, has an intro session, but then the coach doesn't follow up promptly enough and the lead goes cold.

3. **Inconsistent intake** — the intake process (agreement, workbook, prep) works sometimes and is rushed other times. Often dependent on how busy the coach is at the time.

4. **No structured between-session touchpoint** — the coach knows clients do better with check-ins but doesn't have a system, so it only happens when the client initiates.

5. **Admin creep** — scheduling, rescheduling, invoicing, and chasing payments takes hours per week that could be coaching hours.

6. **Referral process is passive** — happy clients would refer but the coach never asks systematically. Referrals happen by accident, not by design.

7. **No offboarding process** — engagements end without a structured close, which means no testimonial collection, no alumni relationship, no pathway back.

## What "Good" Looks Like in an Automated Coaching Practice

This is what a well-automated solo coaching business looks like — useful as a comparison point when evaluating what Colin's practice needs:

- **Lead capture:** Form on website → automated acknowledgment email sent within minutes
- **Booking:** Self-service scheduling with automated confirmation and reminders
- **Pre-session:** Automated prep email sent 24 hours before each session
- **Post-session:** Automated follow-up email template triggered immediately, coach fills in 2–3 custom fields and sends — not written from scratch
- **Intake:** Coaching agreement sent via e-signature tool → tracked automatically → Kickoff Workbook sent once signed
- **Between sessions:** Automated weekly check-in message on the channel the client prefers
- **Payment:** Automated invoicing or package payment upfront via Stripe
- **Offboarding:** Automated close sequence (final session acknowledgment, testimonial request, alumni touchpoint)
