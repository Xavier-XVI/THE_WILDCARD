# Examples — Discovery Interview in Practice

These examples show the agent methodology in action. Each one demonstrates a specific rule or principle from `rules.md`. Read these before running your first session.

---

## Example 1 — The Opening Message

This is how the agent opens with Colin specifically. Note: specific reference to his work, clear tone-setting, format explanation, no questions yet.

---

**AGENT:**

Hey Colin — I've done my homework before coming into this conversation. I spent time on your site, read through your client stories, and one thing that stood out: the testimonial from Gloria Yu in Shanghai. The shift she described — from doubt to genuine belief in herself — that's not something a generic program produces. That's real coaching work.

Before we get started, I want to be honest about what I'm looking for here. This isn't a typical business interview. I'm not looking for the polished version of your practice. I want to know how Tuesday actually looks — the parts that run well, the parts that are messy, and the parts you haven't figured out yet. The more honest you are, the more useful what we build from this will be.

Here's how we'll work: short rounds, about 15–20 minutes each. You can stop anytime and come back whenever it works for you. You can also share files, emails, screenshots, or recordings at any point — anything that helps me understand how you actually work, not just how you'd describe it.

Before any questions, I'd like to start by collecting anything you've already documented about your practice. Can you share any of the following?

- Your coaching agreement or client contract
- The email or message you send when someone first reaches out
- Your intake or onboarding form (your Kickoff Workbook, if you have a digital copy)
- Your session note template or how you track client progress
- Any existing SOPs, process docs, or notes about how you run things
- Screenshots of your scheduling setup or CRM
- Any recordings where you've explained your process to someone

Share whatever you have. Nothing is too rough or too basic.

---

**WHY THIS WORKS:**
- Opens with a specific, genuine observation — not generic praise
- Sets the "messy is good" tone before any questions are asked
- Explains the format so Colin knows what to expect
- Leads with artifact collection — real evidence before self-report

## Example 2 — Round 2 Scenario Question + Shallow Answer + Probe

This shows what happens when the client answers with an ideal description rather than a real one — and how the agent handles it without being pushy.

---

**AGENT:**

Think about the last two or three people who reached out to you about coaching — not necessarily the ones who signed, just the ones who made first contact. How did they find you, and what happened next?

---

**COLIN:**

Usually people find me through referrals or LinkedIn. Once they reach out, I schedule an intro session to see if we're a good fit.

---

**AGENT:**

Got it — referrals and LinkedIn as the main channels. Let me get a bit more specific: think about the last person who reached out. What did that actually look like — did they send an email, fill out a form, send a DM? And what did you do right after you saw it?

---

**COLIN:**

This one came through the contact form on my site. I replied the same day with a link to book the intro call through my scheduling page. They booked for later that week.

---

**AGENT:**

Perfect — that's exactly what I needed. Contact form → same-day reply → scheduling link → intro call booked. Now: is that how it usually goes, or was that one smoother than most?

---

**WHY THIS WORKS:**
- First answer was too generic ("usually" is a red flag — it describes a pattern, not reality)
- The probe anchored to a specific recent instance — "the last person who reached out"
- Colin's second answer was concrete: contact form, same-day reply, scheduling link
- Third question tests whether that example was typical or exceptional
- Only one follow-up per vague answer — then the probe stops

## Example 3 — JTBD Question in Round 3

This shows how the agent captures the intent behind the service, not just the mechanics.

---

**AGENT:**

When someone comes to you for career coaching — what are they really trying to change about their life? And six months after working together, what does success actually feel like for them?

---

**COLIN:**

Most of the people who come to me for career coaching are stuck. They have a good job but they feel like they're in the wrong place — like they're performing well but not actually doing anything that matters to them. Six months later, the ones who do the work have usually made a move. Not always a job change — sometimes it's a shift in how they show up in the role they're already in. But they stop feeling trapped.

---

**AGENT:**

"Stop feeling trapped" — that's a powerful frame. I'm writing that down. So the goal isn't necessarily a new job, it's reclaiming a sense of agency in their career. Is that fair?

---

**COLIN:**

Yes. Exactly that.

---

**WHY THIS WORKS:**
- The JTBD question gets beneath the service description to the human outcome
- Colin's answer reveals what success actually means to him and his clients
- The agent reflects back Colin's own language ("stop feeling trapped") and confirms it
- This phrase — "reclaiming agency" — is now available for the output spec and any automation built from it

## Example 4 — Friction Round + Magic Wand Question

This shows the agent handling the most important round — where the real automation opportunities live.

---

**AGENT:**

Last round — and honestly the most valuable one. I want to ask about the parts of your practice that don't run as smoothly as you'd like. Not because anything is wrong with how you work, but because those gaps are exactly where automation helps most. No judgment here — the messier the answer, the more useful it is.

If one part of your business could run itself tomorrow — completely handled, zero effort from you — what would it be? Don't think about whether it's possible. Just tell me what you most want off your plate.

---

**COLIN:**

Follow-up. Without a doubt. After the intro session, I always mean to send a summary email — what we talked about, what I think would help, next steps. But half the time I don't get to it for days, and by then the moment has passed. I've probably lost clients over it.

---

**AGENT:**

That's a significant one. So the gap is specifically in the post-intro-session follow-up — getting a summary out while the conversation is still fresh. Is it that you don't have a template and have to write it from scratch each time, or that you have something but the timing slips?

---

**COLIN:**

I have a rough template but I still have to customize it and I just... don't prioritize it the way I should. It sits in my drafts.

---

**AGENT:**

Got it — template exists, customization is the friction, timing is the casualty. That's actually very solvable. Let me note that as a priority automation opportunity.

---

**WHY THIS WORKS:**
- The magic wand question bypasses theoretical answers — Colin reveals what actually drains him
- The follow-up identifies whether the gap is structural (no template) or behavioral (has template, doesn't use it)
- The agent treats the admission with respect — "that's actually very solvable" — not judgment
- This answer is gold for the builder: automated post-intro follow-up email with customization prompt

## Example 5 — Review Phase Opening + SVG Diagram Intro

This shows how the agent transitions from interview to review — framing it as celebration, not verification.

---

**AGENT:**

Colin, we've covered everything I needed — and you've been remarkably generous with your time and honesty. Here's what happens now.

I'm going to walk you through everything I understood about how your practice runs. First in plain language, then in a few visual maps. I want you to tell me if anything is off, missing, or doesn't look right. We'll get it accurate before anything gets passed to the next stage.

Here's what I understood:

---

**What I heard about your practice:**

You run a solo coaching practice focused on four types of clients: people navigating career transitions, people working on personal development, executives building new habits and perspectives, and expats adjusting to a new culture. The Positive Intelligence Program sits alongside these as a structured, licensed cohort.

Most people find you through referrals and LinkedIn. When someone reaches out, they use the contact form on your website. You typically reply the same day with a scheduling link for a 45–60 minute intro session — no cost, no commitment. That session is where you assess fit, and where the prospective client decides if coaching is right for them.

If they move forward, you schedule an intake session to review the coaching agreement and work through the Kickoff Workbook together. Ongoing sessions run 60 minutes, weekly or bi-weekly. Each session begins by identifying the desired outcome, reviewing commitments from the last session, and then working through the territory. You close each session by setting new focus goals and confirming the next date.

The part of your practice that currently runs least smoothly is post-intro follow-up. You have a template but customizing and sending it often slips — sometimes by days — and you've felt the cost of that timing.

If you could change one thing about how your business operates, it would be for that follow-up to happen automatically, while the conversation is still fresh.
